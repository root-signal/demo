John McArthur was appointed to his first arbitration in 1994. A decade later, a major utility chose him as its representative on a panel that spent much of 2006 and early 2007 on a dispute over an electricity supply agreement, a tolling agreement. That dispute began as a billion-dollar dispute involving two generating plants, and went to hearing and award as a half-billion-dollar dispute involving one of those plants. When McArthur established his law office in 2008, he did so, in substantial part, to focus on arbitration. He has been devoting most of his time to arbitration for the last dozen years and today he devotes his practice to arbitration.

McArthur began being admitted to major arbitration lists soon after establishing his own office in 2008. The American Arbitration Association made McArthur a neutral in the spring of 2008 for its Large Complex Case, Commercial Case, Oil and Gas, and its National Energy Panel. In 2010, the International Institute for Conflict Prevention and Resolution (CPR) added him as one of its Distinguished Neutrals for its California and Energy, Oil and Gas Panels. The next year, the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA) accepted him as a Public Arbitrator. Today he is often appointed as chair of FINRA panels. He is listed by the international arbitration centers of Hong Kong, Dubai, and Malaysia (the AIAC), and is a listed arbitrator for the London Court of International Arbitration.

As his arbitration practice expanded, McArthur became a Fellow of the College of Commercial Arbitrators, a Member of the National Academy of Distinguished Neutrals, and a Fellow of the Chartered Institute of Arbitrators in London. He was on the team that revised the chapter on Awards in the CCA's Guide to Best Practices in Commercial Arbitration (4th ed. 2017). In 2024 and early 2025, he was one of the five General Editors who managed the Guide's Fifth Edition, published in the early summer 2025. He published a treatise on awards and related topics, including vacatur, in 2022 in THE REASONED ARBITRATION AWARD IN THE UNITED STATES: ITS PROMISE, PROBLEMS, PREPARATION, AND PRESERVATION (Juris Pubs. 2022). McArthur is frequently invited to speak and write about arbitration for fellow arbitrators and for arbitration users. For a list of primarily recent presentations and publications, see Presentations and Publications.


Examples of matters McArthur has arbitrated:

  • Electricity generator/buyer battle over failure to meet terms of long-term electricity tolling agreement, including whether buyer failed to arrange required appropriate distribution facilities and upgrades needed to deliver electricity from plant in Mississippi, whether power company failed to provide adequate assurances, and the measure of damages if breach occurred
  • Buyer/seller contract interpretation dispute over appropriate price for natural gas produced in New Mexico
  • Distributor of manufacturer's fuel-cell batteries for telecom use in California claiming that battery manufacturer breached distribution rights, with counterclaim under sections one and two of the Sherman Act for allegedly anticompetitive conduct by distributor
  • Consultant in sale of oil company suing for commission, seeking declaratory judgment under consulting agreement and damages for unjust enrichment, against company's position that consultant's efforts did not entitle him to any commission
  • Investors in Indiana oil ventures suing operating company for fraud in in multi-year, multi-well investment
  • Broker disputing termination for failure to properly confirm orders before trading bringing breach of contract and good faith, retaliation, fiduciary duty, fraud, wrongful termination, defamation, and related claims
  • Investor in California startup mobile technology company sued to recover funds invested with fraud and related claims that product performance and development schedule were repeatedly misrepresented
  • Investor claim of fraud in startup Oklahoma marijuana growing investment
  • Highly technical field services disputes between major field service company and producer in fields in two of the country's largest producing areas
  • Termination dispute by ousted partner in professional partnership filed against remaining partners over the termination
  • Interest owner in dispute with gold mining company over proper allocation of gold between the parties
  • Large national investment and financial services firm suing former high-level employees for allegedly taking confidential company secrets and business to new firm
  • Investors lawsuit for fraud and related claims against company claiming to use anaerobic digesters to produce biogas in California Central Valley

McArthur brings extensive practical experience to the job of managing arbitrations—a particular advantage when he serves as chair of an arbitration panel—because he has prepared and tried very complex cases over the years. After he joined the Houston law firm Susman Godfrey & McGowan in 1983, Steve Susman asked him to prepare a small payout dispute for trial. After McArthur filed a counterclaim some months later, the case turned into one of the largest take-or- pay cases ever tried. He was one of the two lead trial counsel who secured one of 1988's largest verdicts from a Houston, Texas jury (a $536.2 million verdict, with settlement of $300 million plus property valued at over $100 million). That case was a landmark in “take-or-pay” litigation. In 2004, he was one of two lead trial counsel who secured a $111 million verdict from a jury in Thibodaux, Louisiana (resulting in a $60 million settlement) after trying one of the few oil posted-price cases to go to trial. And in 2012, he presented or cross-examined all witnesses as he shielded the State of Alaska from a claim to reallocate roughly six million barrels of oil, worth over $600 million, in administrative hearings in Anchorage in 2012. (Plaintiff dismissed case after losing first-level appeal).

Most matters McArthur has arbitrated, like those he handled as a trial lawyer, have involved breach of an underlying contract, or of good faith, often coupled with one or more business torts and issues. These have included aiding and abetting, bad faith, conspiracy, constructive trust, conversion, deceptive trade practices, disparagement, economic loss rule, fraud, fraudulent inducement, fraudulent transfer, fiduciary duty, lost profits (and new business defense), negligence, negligent misrepresentation, punitive damages, timing of valuation, tortious interference, unfair business practices, unfair competition, and unjust enrichment. He has been handling contract and business torts disputes, first as a trial lawyer, then as an arbitrator, and exceptions and defenses to these doctrines, for 42 years.

McArthur has been involved with energy matters ever since he began work as a lawyer. On AAA Oil and Gas and National Energy panels; CPR's Oil and Gas Panel, and Fed-Arb Energy Oil Gas & Water panel. Appointments by major and independent oil companies, investors, electric utility, field service company, oilfield equipment inventor, pipeline, and battery company. Experience includes all levels of oil and gas industry: exploration, development, production, transportation, refining, processing, and marketing. Between his former trial work and his work as an arbitrator, he has resolved disputes over billing (partnership, JV, and JOA), AFEs, prospect structure, geology, liquids, reservoir engineering, pricing, deductions, equipment, drilling, production allocation, purchase and sale agreements, take-or-pay agreements, unit agreements, operating agreements, compressors, royalties, and severance taxes, as well as hard-mineral mining, and electricity generation and distribution.

McArthur began his practice at Houston's Susman Godfrey firm in 1983 when the firm focused on antitrust work. His experience covers section-one price fixing (horizontal and vertical), non price restraints, customer restrictions, exclusive dealing, joint-venture arrangements, market division, refusals to deal, and tying; section-two monopoly and attempted monopoly claims; price discrimination; merger claims; Noerr-Pennington; and indirect purchaser issues. Involved in multiple Section One per se and rule of reason claims and Section Two monopolization and attempted monopolization claims. Antitrust experience has involved a very diverse range of industries, from tennis balls to jet fighters, pipelines to gasoline and fuel cell battery pricing and markets.

McArthur has experience in the major forms of business entities and investments: close corporations, joint ventures, partnerships, stock, and other equity structures; issues including business judgment rule, class actions, continuing torts, derivative actions, damages, loan arrangements, ultra vires doctrine, piercing corporate veil, and shareholder liability. Active FINRA arbitrator since 2011, including as panel chair. Securities claims have included claims over broker-dealer rules, corporate misstatements, failures to supervise, insider trading, market manipulation, schemes to defraud under state and federal securities statutes, and violation of FINRA rules.

McArthur has significant academic training, including in the economic concepts surrounding market definition, market power, and the measurement of damages. Holding advanced degrees in economics and public administration as well as his law degree, and a Ph.D. in public policy, he has graduate-level training in the economic and financial concepts used in many complex commercial disputes.
