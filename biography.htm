John McArthur was appointed to his first arbitration in 1994. A decade later, a major utility chose him as its representative on a panel that spent much of 2006 and early 2007 on a dispute over an electricity supply agreement, a tolling agreement. That dispute began as a billion-dollar dispute involving two generating plants, and went to hearing and award as a half-billion-dollar dispute involving one of those plants. When McArthur established his law office in 2008, he did so, in substantial part, to handle more arbitrations--and he has been arbitrating ever since.

The American Arbitration Association listed McArthur as a neutral in the spring of 2008 for its Large Complex Case, Commercial Case, Oil and Gas, and its National Energy Panel. In 2010, the International Institute for Conflict Prevention and Resolution (CPR) added him as one of its Distinguished Neutrals for its California and Energy, Oil and Gas Panels. The next year, the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA) accepted him as a Public Arbitrator. More recently Fed-Arb added him to a number of its panels. With a strong belief in the integrity of our system of securities regulation, he often chairs FINRA panels. He is listed by the international arbitration centers of Hong Kong, Dubai, and Malaysia (the AIAC), and is a listed arbitrator for the London Court of International Arbitration.

McArthur's arbitration expertise has been acknowledged in many ways. He is a Fellow of the College of Commercial Arbitrators, a Member of the National Academy of Distinguished Neutrals, and a Fellow of the Chartered Institute of Arbitrators in London. He was on the team that revised the chapter on Awards in the CCA's Guide to Best Practices in Commercial Arbitration (4th ed. 2017). In 2024 and early 2025, he was one of the five General Editors who managed the Guide's Fifth Edition, published in the early summer 2025. He published a treatise on awards and related topics, including vacatur, in 2022 in THE REASONED ARBITRATION AWARD IN THE UNITED STATES: ITS PROMISE, PROBLEMS, PREPARATION, AND PRESERVATION (Juris Pubs. 2022).

McArthur is frequently invited to speak and write about arbitration for fellow arbitrators and for arbitration users. For a list of primarily recent presentations and publications, see Presentations and Publications on this website.

McArthur brings extensive practical experience to the job of managing arbitrations--a particular advantage when he serves as chair of an arbitration panel--because he has prepared and tried very complex cases over the years. Soon after joining the Houston lawfirm Susman Godfrey & McGowan in 1983, Steve Susman asked him to prepare a small payout dispute for trial. After McArthur filed a counterclaim some months later, the case turned into one of the largest take-or-pay cases ever tried. He was one of the two lead trial counsel who secured one of 1988's largest verdicts from a Houston, Texas jury (a $536.2 million verdict, with settlement of $300 million plus property valued at over $100 million). That case was a landmark in "take-or-pay" litigation.

In 2004, he again was one of two lead trial counsel who secured a $111 million verdict from a jury in Thibodaux, Louisiana (resulting in a $60 million settlement) after trying one of the few oil posted-price cases to go to trial. And in 2012, he presented or cross-examined all witnesses as he shielded the State of Alaska from a claim to reallocate roughly six million barrels of oil, worth over $600 million, in administrative hearing in Anchorage in 2012. (plaintiff dismissed case after losing first-level appeal).

McArthur's experiences in litigation have run the gamut of complex commercial cases. They have covered everything from antitrust and securities disputes to insurance and intellectual property lawsuits, from racketeering and qui tam claims to all the variety of common law contract, fiduciary, partnership, joint venture, fraud and other tort disputes and defenses.

McArthur has significant academic training, including in the economic concepts surrounding market definition, market power, and the measurement of damages. Holding advanced degrees in economics and public administration as well as his law degree, and a Ph.D. in public policy, he has graduate-level training in the economic and financial concepts used in many complex commercial disputes.

McArthur has a broad perspective on managing arbitrations since he has served as an expert and as an arbitrator as well as a trial lawyer. Among his expert testimony is the testimony cited with approval by the Arkansas Supreme Court in Seeco v. Hales, 22 S.W.3d 157 (Ark. 2003).

Major areas of expertise are the following:

Antitrust



McArthur began his practice at Houston's Susman Godfrey firm in 1983 when the firm focused on antitrust work. Experience covers section-one price fixing (horizontal and vertical), nonprice restraints, customer restrictions, exclusive dealing, joint-venture arrangements, market division, refusals to deal, and tying; section-two monopoly and attempted monopoly claims; price discrimination; merger claims; Noerr-Pennington; and indirect purchaser issues. Involved in multiple Section One per se and rule of reason claims and Section Two monopolization and attempted monopolization claims. Antitrust experience has involved a very diverse range of industries, from tennis balls to jet fighters, pipelines to gasoline and fuel cell battery pricing and markets.

Complex Commercial Cases: Contracts and Torts



Throughout McArthur's career, most matters he has handled have usually involved breach of an underlying contract, or of good faith, usually coupled with one or more business torts and issues, including aiding and abetting, bad faith, conspiracy, constructive trust, conversion, deceptive trade practices, disparagement, economic loss rule, fraud, fraudulent inducement, fraudulent transfer, fiduciary duty, lost profits (and new business defense), negligence, negligent misrepresentation, punitive damages, timing of valuation, tortious interference, unfair business practices, unfair competition, and unjust enrichment. He has been applying these doctrines, and exceptions and defenses to them, for 42 years.

Energy, Oil and Gas, Electricity



This is McArthur's largest single trial and arbitration area. On AAA Oil and Gas and National Energy panels; CPR's Oil and Gas Panel, and Fed-Arb Energy Oil Gas & Water panel. Appointments by major and independent oil companies, investors, electric utility, field service company, oilfield equipment inventor, pipeline, and battery company. Experience includes all levels of oil and gas industry: exploration, development, production, transportation, refining, processing, and marketing. Disputes over billing (partnership, JV, and JOA), AFEs, prospect structure, geology, liquids, reservoir engineering, pricing, deductions, equipment, drilling, production allocation, purchase and sale agreements, take-or-pay agreements, unit agreements, operating agreements, compressors, royalties, and severance taxes, as well as hard-mineral mining, and electricity generation and distribution.

Partnerships, Joint Ventures, Other Investment Forms Including Securities Claims



He has extensive experience in the major forms of business entities and investments: close corporations, joint ventures, partnerships, stock, and other equity structures; issues including business judgment rule, class actions, continuing torts, derivative actions, damages, loan arrangements, ultra vires doctrine, piercing corporate veil, and shareholder liability. Active FINRA arbitrator since 2011, often as panel chair. Has handled matters involving stocks, bonds, various funds, and REITs. Securities claims have included claims over registration, broker-dealer rules, corporate misstatements, failures to supervise, insider trading, market manipulation, schemes to defraud under state and federal securities statutes, and violation of FINRA rules.
